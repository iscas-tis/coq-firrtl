Let us assume that $M$ comprises a pair $(pp, ss)$, where $pp$ is a sequence of port declarations and $ss$ is a sequence of statements. 
Our goal is to construct from %the type mapping 
$tm$ and $M$ a mapping $tmap: \range{id}\rightarrow\ftypes \times \{\duplex, \source, \sink\}$. %that records the typing information for the components in $M$. 
%Nevertheless, there is a mismatch between the types in $tm$ and those in $tmap$: While the types in $tm$ are from $\gtypes$, the types in $tmap$ are from $\ftypes$. 
%In the sequel, we shall explain how to tackle this mismatch in the construction of $tmap$. 
%
%We shall construct a function $tmap$ from $\range{id}$ to $\ftypes \times \{\duplex, \source, \sink\}$, where 
Here, in addition to the typing information, the \emph{orientation} of components is also specified. 
There are three types of orientations, i.e., \duplex, \source, and \sink. %The orientations are related to the connect statements. 
In each connect statement $r <= e$, $r$ is a reference and its orientation should be {\duplex} or \sink.
%The rational behind 
The orientations of components and references in $M$ are, 
\begin{itemize}
\item the orientations of wires and registers are $\duplex$, 
\item the orientations of inputs and nodes are \source, 
\item the orientations of outputs are \sink, 
\item the orientation of a reference $t[n]$ are the same as that of $t$, 
\item the orientation of a reference $x.f$ (where $f$ is a field) is  determined from the orientation of $x$ and the flipping status of the field $f$.  
\end{itemize}   

To tackle the mismatch between $tm$ and $tmap$, we introduce a function $\madeexplicit$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:made-explicit}). $\madeexplicit$ takes three arguments $(ft, id, tm)$, where $ft \in \ftypes$, $id \in \gidset_M$, and $tm$ is a type mapping and %Intuitively, $\madeexplicit$ utilizes $id$ and $tm$ to determine 
prescribes the bit widths in $ft$ (i.e. the widths for the ground types $(\uintimp, 0)$ and $(\sintimp, 0)$), resulting in a new type $newt$ where all the widths are explicit (i.e. non-zero). 

To this end, $\madeexplicit$ uses a function $\sizeofftype$ to calculate the number of ground types included in a type or a sequence of fields. %(see Fig.~\ref{fig:size-list-ftype-fields} for its definition) 
Formally, $\sizeofftype$ is recursively defined as follows. 
\tl{to see how to improve this}. 
%
\begin{align*}
	\sizeofftype ((\gtype, gt)) & =1\\
	\sizeofftype ((\vtype, t'[m])) & =  \sizeofftype(t')*m\\
	\sizeofftype ((\btype, \vec{f})) & =  \sizeoffields(\vec{f})\\
	\sizeoffields (\nil) & = 0 \\
	\sizeoffields((\_, \_, t')\ \vec{f}') & = \sizeofftype(t')+ \sizeoffields(\vec{f}') 
\end{align*}

%It also calls a function $\typeeq$ to check the equivalence between two ground types, one from $M$ and the other one from $tm$. For instance, in $\typeeq$, two types $(\uintimp, n_1)$ and $(\uintimp, n_2)$ are equivalent regardless of $n_1$ and  $n_2$, and the (abstract) $\reset$ type from $M$ is equivalent to both $(\uint, 1)$ and $\asyncreset$ from $tm$. (Note that according to the syntax of {\lofirrtl} in Fig.~\ref{fig-lofirrtl-synt}, the $\reset$ type does not appear in {\lofirrtl}. )

We also define a relation $\typeeq$ to specify the equivalence between two ground types, one from $M$ and the other one from $tm$. Namely, 
%
\[
\begin{array}{l c l}
\typeeq & := & \{((\uint, n), (\uint, n)) \mid n  \in \Nat \} \cup \{((\sint, n), (\sint, n)) \mid n  \in \Nat \}\ \cup \\
&& \{((\uintimp, n_1), (\uintimp, n_2)) \mid n_1, n_2 \in \Nat\}\ \cup  \\
%		
&& \{((\sintimp, n_1), (\sintimp, n_2)) \mid n_1, n_2 \in \Nat\}\ \cup  \\
%		
&&  \{(\clock, \clock)\} \cup \{(\reset, (\uint, 1)), (\reset,\asyncreset)\}\ \cup \\
& & \{(\asyncreset,\asyncreset)\}.
\end{array}
\]   
It is easy to observe that $\typeeq$ is not symmetric, thus not an equivalence relation.  As a result, the aforementioned  ``equivalence'' in $\typeeq$ is just an intuitive way of saying the compatibility between the two ground types,  
Note that in $\typeeq$, two types $(\uintimp, n_1)$ and $(\uintimp, n_2)$ are equivalent regardless of $n_1$ and  $n_2$. Moreover, the (abstract) $\reset$ type from $M$ is equivalent to both $(\uint, 1)$ and $\asyncreset$ from $tm$. (Recall that the $\reset$ type does not appear in {\lofirrtl} according to the syntax of {\lofirrtl} in Fig.~\ref{fig-lofirrtl-synt}.)

The function $\madeexplicit(ft, id, tm)$ are defined by the rules in Fig.~\ref{fig:made-explicit}. 
\begin{itemize}
\item The rule {\madeexplicit: \gtype} deals with the case $ft = (\gtype, gt)$. In this case, $\madeexplicit$ simply returns the ground type in $tm(id)$. 
%
\item The rule {\madeexplicit: \vtype} deals with the case $ft = (\vtype, t[n])$. In this case, $\madeexplicit$ first computes $t' = \madeexplicit(t, id, tm)$, then returns $t'[n]$. Moreover, it checks that in $vm$, the $n$ ground-type sequences in $tm$ corresponding to the $n$ copies of $t'$ are all equal, that is, let $id = (v,m)$ and $sz = \sizeofftype(t')$, then $(tm(v, m+0), \ldots, tm(v, m+sz-1)) = (tm(v, m+sz), \ldots, tm(v, m+2sz-1)) = \cdots = (tm(v, m+(n-1)sz), \ldots, tm(v, m+nsz-1))$. 
%
\item The rule {\madeexplicit: \btype} deals with the case $ft = (\btype, \vec{f})$. In this case, $\madeexplicit$ calls $\madeexplicitfields(\vec{f}, id, tm)$ and returns its output.  
\end{itemize}
The function $\madeexplicitfields(\vec{f}, id, tm)$ recursively computes the output as follows. If $\vec{f} = \nil$, then it returns $(\btype, \nil)$. Otherwise, let $\vec{f} = (x, b, t)\ \myvec{f'}$. Then it calls $\madeexplicit(t, id, tm)$ to get $t'$ and recursively calls $\madeexplicitfields(\myvec{f'}, id, tm)$ to get $\myvec{f''}$. Finally, it returns $newt = (x, b, t')\ \myvec{f''}$. 

Note that the rules in Fig.~\ref{fig:made-explicit} are chosen to be incomplete in purpose to avoid the tediousness. For instance, in the rule {\madeexplicit: \vtype}, if the $n$ ground-type sequences in $tm$ corresponding to the $n$ copies of $t'$ are not equal, then $\madeexplicit$ returns the special value $\svnone$.

The mapping $tmap$ is constructed by the procedure $\portsstmtstmap(pp, ss, tm)$, which calls the procedure $\portstmap(pp, tm)$ to compute $pmap$, then calls $\stmtstmap((pmap, pmap), ss, tm)$ to get $tmap$ (see Figure~\ref{fig:ports-stmts-tmap}). Intuitively, $\portstmap(pp, tm)$ constructs a mapping from the port declarations $pp$ and $tm$, and $stmts\_tmap((pmap, pmap), ss, tm)$ constructs a pair $(tmap, scope)$ from $(pmap, pmap)$, the statement sequence $ss$, and $tm$. Note that $scope$ is introduced to check that the component references in $ss$ do not violate the scoping constraints brought upon by the \prettyll{when} statements in it. The procedures $\portstmap(pp, tm)$ and $\stmtstmap((pmap, pmap), ss, tm)$ are defined by the rules in Fig.~\ref{fig:ports-stmts-tmap}.
To help the readers understand these rules, let us explain the rules {\portstmap: input} and {\stmtstmap: $\neq$\nil}. 
\begin{itemize}
\item In the rule {ports\_tmap: input}, suppose $pp = input\ x: t\ pp'$. Then $made\_explicit$ is called to instantiate $t$ into $newt$ by $tm$, where all the bit widths are explicit. (The definition of $made\_explicit$ will be given in Fig.~\ref{fig:made-explicit}.) Let $tmap'$ be the mapping that is obtained by recursively calling $ports\_tmap$ on $(pp', tm)$. Then it is required that $tmap'(id(x)) = none$, that is, $x$ is not declared in $pp'$. Finally, $tmap$ is obtained from $tmap'$ by assigning $(newt, \source)$ to $id(x)$.  
%
\item In the rule {stmts\_tmap: $\neq$nil}, suppose $ss = s\ ss'$. Then $stmt\_tmap(s, tm)$ is called to compute $(tmap'', scope'')$. Moreover, $stmts\_tmap$ is recursively called on $((tmap'', scope''), ss', tm)$ to compute $(tmap', scope')$. 
\end{itemize}
Note that to avoid the tediousness, we omit some rules for $ports\_tmap$ and $stmts\_tmap$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:ports-stmts-tmap}.  For instance, if $tmap'(id(x)) = none$ in the rule {ports\_tmap: input} is changed to $tmap'(id(x)) \neq none$, then $ports\_tmap(pp, tm) = none$. Intuitively, we omit the rules corresponding to the abnormal situations where the result is $none$.
