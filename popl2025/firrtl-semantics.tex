For a ground type $t = (\tau, n) \in \{\mbox{\prettyll{UInt}}, \mbox{\prettyll{SInt}}\} \times \Nat$, $n$ is the \emph{width} of $t$, denoted by $wd(t)$.

Fix a {\hifirrtl} module $M$ where we assume that $M$ consists of a sequence of port declarations followed by statements. %$M = (pp, ss)$, where $pp$ is a sequence of port declarations and $ss$ is a sequence of statements. 
Moreover, for convenience, we assume an \emph{injective} identifier function $id$, which maps from the name of a component (e.g., wire, node, register) in $M$ to a natural number. %Note that $id$ is an injective function. Later on, 
We shall use identifiers (instead of names) to refer to the components in $M$. 

In the sequel, we define a concept of \emph{module graphs} to define the semantics of {\hifirrtl} programs. Intuitively, a module graph $G$ for $M$ represents an instantiation of $M$, where all the implicit widths are made explicit. Moreover, the vertices of $G$ represent the components of ground type that are obtained from $M$ by flattening the components of aggregate type. 

As a result, the vertices of a module graph for $M$ are of the form $(a, o) \in \Nat \times \Nat$, where  
$(a, o)$ is one of the following forms, 
%each component $cnt$ of ground type in $M$ is associated with an identifier $(a, o)$, where
\begin{itemize}
\item $a = id(cnt)$ where $cnt$ is a component of vector type, and $o$ is an index in the vector, 
% 
\item $a = id(cnt)$ where $cnt$ is a component of bundle type, and $o$ is the offset of a field of $cnt$ of ground type,
%is a sub-component of some other component $cnt'$ of an aggregate type, then $a \in \Nat$ is the identifier of $cnt'$ \tl{there is a bit issue here as the identifier of $cnt'$ is a pair?} and $o$ represents the offset of $cnt$ in $cnt'$, 
\item $a = id(cnt)$ where $cnt$ is a component of ground type, and $o = 0$.
\end{itemize}

For a component of bundle type, the offsets of its fields of ground type are obtained by a depth-first search over its fields. For instance, for the bundle type 
 
 \prettyll{output io : {flip enable : UInt<1>, calc : UInt<3>}}, the offsets of \prettyll{enable} and \prettyll{calc} are $0$ and $1$ respectively.

We consider a finite set $\sorts = \{ \inport, \outport, \wire, \node, \register\}$ of \emph{sorts}.

A \emph{vertex type} is either \tl{the name "type" is overloaded, can be change to something else?}
\begin{itemize}
\item $(s, t)$ with $s \in \{\inport, \outport, \wire, \node\}$ and $t \in \gtypes$,  
\item or $(\register, t, e_1, e_2, e_3, b)$ such that $t \in \gtypes$, $e_1, e_2, e_3 \in \exps$ and $b\in \{0,1\}$. (Intuitively, $e_1$ represents a clock, $e_2$ represents a reset clock, and $e_3$ represents the reset value, and $b$ is a Boolean value  that denotes whether the reset is asynchronous or not).  
\end{itemize}
Let $\vtypes$ denote the set of vertex types. Moreover, for convenience, we assume that $\vtypes$ includes two additional elements, i.e. $none$ and $undefined$. 

A \emph{vertex map} is a function $vm: \idset_M \rightarrow \vtypes$ with a finite domain. 

A \emph{connection tree} is a function $ct: \idset_M \rightarrow \exps$ with a finite domain. 

%A \emph{vertex} is a pair $\idset \times \vtypes$.

\begin{definition}[Module graph]\label{def-mg}
	A \emph{module graph} $G$ is a pair $(vm, ct)$ where $vm$ is a vertex map and $ct$ is a connection tree. 
\end{definition}
